# Mechanism: Nacre (Fracture Toughness)

## Biological Context
Nacre, also known as mother-of-pearl, is the inner shell layer of mollusks (*Conchifera*). It is 3,000 times tougher than its constituent mineral (calcite) alone.

## Physical Principle
- **Brick-and-Mortar Structure**: Nacre is composed of hexagonal platelets of aragonite (the "bricks") held together by a thin, flexible organic matrix of chitin and proteins (the "mortar").
- **Crack Deflection**: When a crack starts, the layered structure forces it to take a tortuous, zig-zag path, significantly increasing the energy required for the crack to propagate.
- **Platelet Sliding**: Under stress, the organic mortar allows the mineral platelets to slide past one another. This sliding dissipates enormous amounts of energy through friction and localized plastic deformation.
- **Mineral Bridges**: Microscopic bridges connect adjacent platelets, providing additional resistance to pull-out and enhancing the overall stiffness of the material.

## Engineering Applications
- **Impact-Resistant Composites**: Developing lightweight yet tough materials for bulletproof armor and blast protection.
- **Advanced Ceramics**: Designing brittle-core ceramics with a layered architecture to prevent catastrophic failure in aerospace and industrial applications.
- **Bio-medical Implants**: Creating bone substitutes that mimic the hierarchical toughness of natural bone and shell.

## Keywords
Nacre, Fracture Toughness, Brick-and-Mortar, Crack Deflection, Platelet Sliding, Composite Materials.
