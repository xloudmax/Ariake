# Mechanism: Whale Fin Tubercles (Passive Flow Control)

## Biological Context
Humpback whales (*Megaptera novaeangliae*) possess large, rounded bumps called **tubercles** on the leading edges of their pectoral flippers. These structures allow the whale to maintain lift and maneuverability at steep angles of bank and low speeds while hunting.

## Physical Principle
- **Vortex Generation**: Tubercles act as passive vortex generators. As water flows over the leading edge, the bumps channel the flow into narrow streams, creating counter-rotating vortices.
- **Boundary Layer Stabilization**: These vortices re-energize the boundary layer by mixing high-momentum fluid from the outer flow into the slower-moving fluid near the surface.
- **Delay of Stall**: This prevents premature flow separation (stall), allowing the foil to maintain lift at much higher angles of attack (up to 40% increase in stall angle) than a smooth-edged foil.

## Engineering Applications
- **Wind Turbine Blades**: Incorporating tubercles on the leading edge to improve energy extraction in turbulent or low-wind conditions.
- **Propellers and Fans**: Reducing noise and cavitation by stabilizing the flow across the blade surface.
- **Aviation**: Enhancing the lift-to-drag ratio and stall recovery of UAVs and small aircraft wings.

## Keywords
Passive Flow Control, Vortex Generator, Boundary Layer, Angle of Attack, Aerodynamics, Stall Delay.
