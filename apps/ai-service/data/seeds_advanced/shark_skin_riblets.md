# Mechanism: Shark Skin Riblets (Drag Reduction)

## Biological Context
Sharks (*Selachimorpha*) are capable of high-speed cruising and rapid bursts while swimming, despite their large size. Their skin is covered in microscopic tooth-like scales called **dermal denticles**.

## Physical Principle
- **Riblet Geometry**: The denticles have longitudinal V-grooves or "riblets" aligned with the flow direction.
- **Vortex Lifting**: The riblets lift the large-scale turbulent vortices away from the wall, minimizing the contact between high-speed fluid and the skin surface.
- **Transverse Dampening**: By restricting the lateral movement of boundary-layer vortices, riblets prevent the formation of "streaks" that characterize energy-intensive turbulent bursts.
- **Skin Friction Reduction**: This manipulation results in a net reduction of skin-friction drag by 5-10% compared to a smooth surface.

## Engineering Applications
- **Aviation**: Fine riblet films applied to aircraft fuselages and wings to improve fuel economy during long-haul flights (e.g., Airbus test flights).
- **High-Performance Swimwear**: Competitive swimsuits (like the Speedo LZR series) that mimic riblet structures to reduce hydrodynamic drag.
- **Wind Energy**: Improving the efficiency of large turbine blades by reducing boundary layer turbulence.
